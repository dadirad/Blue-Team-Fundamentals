What Is the Blue Team?
  • Blue Team = defenders who detect, respond to, and learn from cyber attacks
  • Day-to-day: monitoring, triage, investigation, containment, reporting, and improvement
  • Goal: reduce attacker dwell time and limit business impact
Good blue team analysis is not only about spotting something odd — it is about explaining why it matters and what should happen next.

What Is Incident Response?
  • IR is a structured way to handle suspected or confirmed security incidents
  • Goal: limit impact fast while preserving evidence and restoring operations
  • IR produces improvements — better detections, better logging, stronger controls

Investigation Approach
  • Start broad: validate event types and basic counts
  • Pick an entity: choose one host to investigate first
  • Pivot for evidence: domain, URI, destination IP and port
  • Build a timeline: order events to explain the sequence
  • Document: confidence level, containment action, detection improvement
Analyst habit: move from lead → context → conclusion. Do not jump from one interesting indicator directly to a final claim.

Event Types: Each Tells a Different Story
DNS

What domain did the host try to resolve?

HTTP

What web host and path were requested?

TLS

What server name was presented during encrypted traffic?

CONN

Who connected to whom, on what port, and how often?

Why this matters:   A rare domain can be a lead  ·  A payload-like URI can suggest delivery  ·  Regular connection intervals can suggest beaconing  ·  No single field is enough — correlation is the skill.

What to Look For

Rare DNS Domain

Uncommon domains can point to low-prevalence infrastructure. Rare does not equal malicious, but it deserves a second look.

Payload-Like HTTP Path

Paths such as download, install, update, or extensions like .exe or .ps1 may indicate delivery or staging.

Repeated Outbound Cadence

Regular intervals to the same destination can indicate beaconing or recurring task behavior.

External Destinations

A host that talks to many external IPs or unfamiliar infrastructure can stand out during triage.

Correlation across event types: Confidence increases when the same host shows suspicious DNS, HTTP, and repeated connection behavior near the same time.

Common Beginner Errors

Treating the top score as proof

The ranking helps you prioritize, but you still need to validate with evidence.

Calling every rare domain malicious

Rare only means uncommon in this dataset. Context matters.

Looking at one field in isolation

A URI or domain is stronger when tied to the same host and time window.

Ignoring timing

A timeline helps distinguish related activity from unrelated noise.

Writing findings without confidence language. State what you know, what you infer, and what you still need to verify.

Lab Tasks 4–6: Investigate
Now turn host-level suspicion into a defensible finding.

Task 4 — Find Suspicious Artifacts

Identify rare domains, unusual web paths, or suspicious destinations associated with the host.

Task 5 — Check for Beaconing

Look for repeated outbound connections to the same destination and note the interval pattern.

Task 6 — Build the Timeline

Sequence the events so you can explain what likely happened first, next, and last.

Deliverable checklist:   Likely compromised host  ·  Most suspicious domain, path, or IP  ·  Why the evidence matters  ·  One containment action  ·  One detection improvement

Writing Your Incident Summary
Keep it short, evidence-based, and easy for another analyst to follow.

Suggested Outline

  • State the host you believe is most suspicious
  • Describe the most important suspicious artifacts
  • Explain the timing or repeated pattern that strengthens your conclusion
  • Recommend containment and one detection improvement

Confidence Language

  • High: multiple indicators align across host, destination, and timing
  • Medium: evidence is suspicious but has plausible benign explanations
  • Low: interesting lead, but not enough correlation yet
Be specific about what you observed versus what you infer.

Key Takeaways
  • Network logs tell you where a host talked — DNS, HTTP, TLS, and CONN each reveal a different piece.
  • Rare DNS + payload URI + beaconing together build a strong case. One signal alone is just a lead.
  • Timing matters. A timeline separates related activity from background noise.
  • Confidence language — high, medium, low — keeps your findings honest and actionable.
  • Your incident summary is your product. Evidence first, inference second, action third.
